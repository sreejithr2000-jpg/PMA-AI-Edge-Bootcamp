Finding 1

Black Friday AOV is 50% higher than normal — despite being a discount event

Electronics: $100.40 → $150.82  |  Home Goods: $49.70 → $70.60  |  Both categories lifted within themselves
Explained ✓
The Anomaly
+50.2%
Electronics AOV uplift on Black Friday
Normal: $100.40  →  BF: $150.82
Home Goods: $49.70 → $70.60 (+42%)

Counterintuitive: discounts should compress AOV, not expand it.
Hypotheses Tested
H1A — Bigger basket sizes: BF shoppers buy more items per cart
If a cart has 3× items at the same unit price, revenue/purchase rises even with discounts per item.
Likely Confirmed
H1B — Category mix shift: Electronics dominates BF
BF brings more Electronics (higher price) buyers → aggregate AOV lifts via mix effect.
Partial — explains ~15%
H1C — Pent-up demand: BF unlocks large planned purchases
Consumers wait for BF to buy high-value items. These are intentional large orders, not impulse buys.
Confirmed — both categories lift

Normal Electronics share ≈ 60.3% → mix shift explains only +3pp, not the 50% AOV jump. The within-category lift proves H1C dominates.

PM Implication

Black Friday isn't primarily a discount-driven event for your store — it's a high-intent, large-basket event. Customers were saving up to buy Electronics. This means your BF strategy should focus on bundle promotions and high-ticket items rather than deep discounts on low-AOV goods. The AOV cliff post-BF ($150 → $83) is the real number to watch — it reflects the return to everyday casual shopping.

PM Implication — Two separate acquisition strategies needed

  • New customers: High AOV but risky — focus on size guides, product fit tools, and clear return policies to reduce the 12.99% return rate eating into margin.

Finding 4

Checkout conversion locks at 82–88% regardless of traffic volume — across every single day

During BF, visitors tripled. Checkout conversion stayed 83–89%. This should not happen without a structural explanation.
Explained — quality gate effect ✓
The Anomaly
±3pp
Total checkout conversion range across all 14 days
Normal traffic: 82–87% checkout → purchase
BF peak (3× visitors): 83–89% checkout → purchase

If BF attracted casual browsers, conversion should drop — it didn't.
Hypotheses
H4A — Add-to-Cart acts as a quality gate eliminating casual browsers early
Only ~8.8% of visitors add to cart even during BF. By checkout stage, the funnel has already filtered to high-intent buyers. Volume doesn't affect late-funnel quality.
Confirmed — ATC rate also stable at 8–10%
H4B — BF traffic is actually higher-intent than normal traffic
BF visitors came specifically to buy. They're more likely to complete a purchase than casual Nov browsers. Volume increase is intentional shoppers, not random browsers.
Confirmed — BF purchase rate holds at 4.2–5.3%

PM Implication — Your funnel quality is robust, but ATC is the leverage point

The checkout→purchase conversion is essentially fixed at ~85%. If you want more revenue, improving checkout conversion is not your lever — it's already near-optimal. The real lever is the Add-to-Cart rate (8.8% of visitors). A 1pp lift in ATC rate would add ~820 more add-to-carts per day on a normal day, flowing through at 85% checkout efficiency to ~580 additional purchases. That's your highest-ROI optimization target.
